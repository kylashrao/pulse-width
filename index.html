# Latency Status
What it checks

Three signals, one verdict

Speed alone doesn't tell you if a site holds up. We look at how fast, how consistent, and how reliably it responds — because a server that's fast once and times out the next is worse than one that's steadily mediocre.

Response speed

Time to first byte and total round trip, measured directly by your browser's network stack — not a third-party estimate.

Stability under repetition

We send a run of consecutive requests and chart the spread. A server that swings wildly between fast and slow shows up immediately on the strip.

Availability

Every failed, timed-out, or non-2xx response counts against the success rate, so an unreliable host can't hide behind a good average.

How it works

No server in the middle

Most "speed test" tools route your request through their own infrastructure first. This one doesn't — your browser talks to the target directly, so the numbers reflect what your visit would actually feel like.

1

You enter a URL

No account, no install. The address bar is the whole interface.

2

Your browser fires the requests

A sequence of real HTTP requests goes out from your machine, timed with the browser's native Performance API.

3

We plot every result

Each ping lands on the stability strip as it completes — pass, slow, or failed — building the picture live.

4

You get a plain verdict

Median speed, jitter, and success rate roll up into one of three calls: solid, shaky, or unreliable.

Why the numbers can differ from other tools: services like WebPageTest run from dedicated lab machines on controlled networks and can read deep browser-internal timing because they load the page in a full instrumented browser they control. A page tested from your own browser is bound by the same-origin policy — most sites don't send the CORS headers that would let us see DNS, TCP, and TLS time separately for a cross-origin request. When that detail isn't available, we measure total elapsed time end-to-end instead and say so plainly, rather than inventing a breakdown we can't actually see.

About

A meter, not a marketing claim.

Pulsewidth exists because most "is this site fast?" tools answer a question nobody asked, from a vantage point nobody visits from. Here's what we're actually trying to do instead.

Why this exists

Most performance dashboards are built to make a number go up — a Lighthouse score, a synthetic grade, a badge you can put in a README. Those numbers are useful for what they measure, but they're measured from a lab: a fixed data center, a controlled network, a browser instrumented in ways your actual visitors' browsers never are.

Pulsewidth measures something narrower and, we think, more honest: what happens when your own browser, on your own connection, asks a server for a page — twelve times in a row. No lab. No synthetic throttling profile standing in for a real network. Just repeated, real requests, timed plainly.

What "benchmark" means here

We don't grade sites against an industry curve or assign letter grades. A benchmark, to us, just means: here is the same test, run the same way, every time — so you can compare a site against itself over time, or compare two sites under the same conditions, without us deciding what "good" means on your behalf.

The verdict labels — Solid, Shaky, Unreliable, Unreachable — are deliberately plain. They roll up median speed, consistency (jitter), and success rate into one read, but the underlying numbers are always shown too, so you're never just trusting our adjective.

What we won't pretend to do

Browsers enforce a security boundary called the same-origin policy. It means a page running in your browser can't always see the fine-grained timing breakdown (DNS time, TCP handshake, TLS negotiation) for a site it doesn't control — that detail is only exposed when the target site explicitly opts in via CORS headers, and most don't.

When that detail isn't available, we say so directly in the results rather than inventing numbers that look precise but aren't real. We'd rather hand you an honest "we can't see that part" than a confident-looking chart built on a guess.

Who built this

Pulsewidth is an independent project, built and maintained by Kailash Rao Shivaji. It's part of a small set of self-directed tools and learning projects rather than a venture-backed product — which is also why it has no tracking, no accounts, and no plan to ever require either.

Privacy

The short version: there's almost nothing to collect.

Pulsewidth has no accounts, no analytics, and no server that sees your test results. This section explains exactly what that means in practice.

Last updated: June 2026

What Pulsewidth does when you run a test

When you enter a URL and click "Run benchmark," your browser sends a series of HTTP requests directly to that URL. This happens entirely client-side — there is no Pulsewidth server sitting in the middle, relaying your request, or logging which sites you've tested. The JavaScript runs locally in your browser tab, and the results are rendered locally in that same tab.

If you close the tab or navigate away, the results are gone. We don't have a copy, because we never received one.

What we do not collect

  • We don't use cookies for tracking or advertising.
  • We don't run analytics scripts that record your visits, clicks, or test history.
  • We don't require — or offer — account creation, so there's no profile tied to your usage.
  • We don't store the URLs you test, the results they produce, or your IP address in any database, because no such database exists for this purpose.

What the sites you test might see

This is the one place where "we collect nothing" doesn't mean "nothing happens." When your browser sends a request to a third-party URL you've entered, that site sees the request the same way it would see a normal visit — your IP address, your browser's user agent, and standard request headers, exactly as if you'd typed the URL into a new tab yourself. Pulsewidth doesn't add any additional tracking to those requests, but it also can't remove the visibility a website normally has into who's visiting it.

In short: testing a site is, from that site's point of view, indistinguishable from visiting it. Keep that in mind if you're testing a URL you'd rather not have associated with your IP address.

Fonts and external resources

This page loads typefaces from Google Fonts. Loading a font from a third-party CDN means your browser makes a request to that CDN, which — like any web request — is visible to the company operating it. We don't control that exchange beyond choosing to use the fonts, and we don't add any additional tracking on top of it.

The contact form

The form in our Contact us section doesn't submit to a server we operate. It opens your own email client with a pre-filled message, using your browser's native mailto: handling. Nothing you type into that form is transmitted anywhere until you choose to send the resulting email yourself.

Changes to this policy

If how Pulsewidth works ever changes in a way that affects this section — for instance, if a future version added optional accounts or server-side history — this page would be updated to reflect that before such a feature shipped, not after.
